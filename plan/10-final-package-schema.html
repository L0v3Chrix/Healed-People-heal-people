What this schema does

Status: interview closed, gates explicit

This schema was used to verify the completed final documents 12 through 21 — the interview is closed, and decision-dependent regulated and vendor matters are represented as explicit release gates.

Each final requirement will carry:

  • Requirement/decision ID
  • Statement
  • Source: reviewed asset, confirmed interview answer, or professional determination
  • Owner
  • Acceptance evidence
  • Dependency
  • Target phase: definition, prototype, beta readiness, beta, campaign readiness, campaign, or post-campaign

1. Pre-Design Review — name every truth before designing

The PDR is where honesty gets written down

Before any design or build scope moves, the PDR must capture what we believe, who it serves, and what we still don't know.

Required contents

  • Product thesis and dignity principles
  • Problem/opportunity statement
  • Confirmed audiences, users, buyers, operators, and beneficiaries
  • Primary beta proof and reality level
  • Approved operating model and value exchange
  • Governance/entity/authority model
  • Participant safety and consent model
  • Approved money-flow description
  • Physical/digital system boundary
  • Existing evidence and prototype maturity
  • Assumptions explicitly accepted
  • Open professional determinations
  • Key risks and mitigations
  • Design principles and approved brand direction
  • PDR exit criteria, approvers, and approval date
PDR completion test

No downstream design or build scope depends on an unnamed decision owner, unsupported public claim, or unlabeled illustrative value.

2. Project Scope — draw the boundary and defend it

Every deliverable earns its place

Scope is a promise about what will exist, what won't, and who answers for each piece.

Required contents

  • Objective and measurable outcomes
  • In-scope workflows and deliverables
  • Explicit out-of-scope list
  • Beta geography, dates, duration, and quantities
  • Required roles and responsibility assignment
  • Workstreams and work breakdown
  • Dependencies and decision gates
  • Milestones and review gates
  • Budget boundary and approval rules
  • Acceptance, continue, revise, pause, and stop criteria
  • Change-control process
  • Handoff, ownership, and maintenance expectations
Scope completion test

Every deliverable has an owner, acceptance condition, dependency, target date/window, and source decision.

3. Technical Requirements — make every transition accountable

The system spec that money and people can trust

The technical specification must cover the whole machine — from trust boundaries to backup and recovery — with special weight on anything that moves money or touches a participant.

Required contents

  • System context and trust boundaries
  • Approved roles and permission matrix
  • Recipient, participant, partner, business, sponsor, operator, finance, support, and auditor workflows as applicable
  • Sticker/batch/code lifecycle
  • Reward/offer/claim/redemption lifecycle
  • Ledger/obligation/disbursement lifecycle
  • Definitive state transitions, timeouts, reversals, disputes, and corrections
  • Data model and financial invariants
  • API/integration contracts
  • Authentication and authorization
  • Fraud prevention, review, and appeal
  • Privacy/data inventory, minimization, retention, access, export, and deletion
  • Security, secrets, logging, incident response, backup, and recovery
  • Accessibility, language, device/browser, bandwidth, and non-digital accommodation
  • Performance, reliability, scalability, observability, and support targets
  • Migration, seed, environment, release, and rollback requirements
  • Testing strategy and requirement-to-test traceability
  • Beta operational readiness and technical acceptance criteria
Specification completion test

Every money-moving and participant-impacting transition is authorized, idempotent, auditable, reversible through a correction path, testable, and assigned to a human escalation owner.

4. Tech Stack — justify every layer

Tools chosen for the workflow, not the hype

Each layer of the stack must trace to a confirmed workflow need and carry an owner, a cost assumption, and an exit path.

Required contents for each layer

  • Confirmed workflow need
  • Recommended product/service and version policy
  • Alternatives considered
  • Selection rationale tied to interview constraints
  • Account/data/billing owner
  • Security/privacy/accessibility implications
  • Cost assumption and usage driver
  • Setup and environment model
  • Backup/export/vendor-exit path
  • Training/skill requirement

Layers to evaluate

🖥️Web application and UI

⚙️API/server execution

🗄️Database, authentication, storage, and scheduled work

🔳QR/serialization and print-data generation

✉️Communications

📊Analytics and reporting

🔔Monitoring/error tracking

🤖AI, only if retained

💸Payment/disbursement/accounting, only after approval

📇CRM/email and support

🚀Deployment/CI/CD/source control

📦Campaign/fulfillment integrations

5. Required Assets — inventory everything the project must show

From brand files to consent forms

Every asset the project needs gets a register entry — with rights, approvals, and accessibility built in from the start.

Asset register fields

  • Asset name and purpose
  • Audience/channel
  • Existing, revise, replace, or create
  • Source file and format
  • Owner/creator
  • Rights/release status
  • Approved claims/data
  • Accessibility variants
  • Print/web/video specifications
  • Dependency and due window
  • Acceptance approver

Asset groups

  • Brand identity and usage guide
  • Product/sticker/packaging production files
  • QR/serialization data and print proofs
  • Role-based product UX and content
  • Operational forms, SOPs, training, consent, and support materials
  • Legal/privacy/terms/financial disclosures
  • Reporting and impact templates
  • Campaign page, graphics, video, photography, audio, captions, transcript, press, email, social, FAQ, risk, and update assets
  • Reward/fulfillment production and support assets

6. Platforms — own every account on purpose

No orphan logins, no accidental dependencies

Every approved platform gets named owners, a data map, and a way out.

For every approved platform, state:

  • Confirmed workflow
  • Organization owner and backup admin
  • Billing owner
  • Roles/access policy
  • Data processed and processors
  • Environment/project naming
  • Integration boundary
  • Export/backup/offboarding process
  • Acceptance check
Prototype presence is not a requirement

No platform is required solely because it appears in a prototype.

7. Subscriptions and Services — pay only for confirmed work

Every recurring dollar has a job

Services make the list only when a confirmed workflow needs them — and each one carries its cost, its limits, and its cancellation path.

Required fields

  • Service and approved tier
  • Purpose
  • Account/billing owner
  • Monthly/annual/usage cost basis
  • Nonprofit/fiscal-sponsor eligibility if applicable
  • Usage limit and overage behavior
  • Contract/renewal/cancellation date
  • Data/privacy/security review
  • Backup/export/vendor-exit route
  • Cost-monitoring and approval threshold
Inclusion rule

Include only services required by confirmed workflows.

8. Crowdfunding Plan — promise only what the evidence supports

The campaign chapter of the schema

The crowdfunding plan must be costed, honest, and operationally ready — from the first-48-hour plan to refund and shortfall rules.

Required contents

  • Campaign purpose and proof level
  • Eligibility/entity/fiscal-sponsor position
  • Priority audience and evidence
  • Platform and funding-model decision
  • Costed minimum, target, contingency, and stretch logic
  • Use-of-funds model
  • Honest backer promise
  • Reward architecture or explicit no-reward model
  • Campaign narrative and page outline
  • Proof/trust package
  • Asset production plan
  • Pre-launch audience-building plan
  • Launch calendar and first-48-hour operating plan
  • Press/community/business/creator outreach
  • Paid promotion boundary if approved
  • Fulfillment plan and landed costs
  • Backer support and update cadence
  • Risks, delays, refunds, shortfall, overfunding, and scope-change rules
  • Claims, partner, participant, creative, budget, and launch approvals
  • Campaign acceptance/release checklist
Campaign completion test

The published page cannot visually or verbally imply a higher proof level, confirmed partner, tax status, participant payment, impact outcome, reward availability, or delivery certainty than documented evidence supports.

the campaign page only says what we can prove — that's the whole trick.

9. Tools and Setup — verify before you claim

Installation guides that tell the truth

Every tool the project depends on gets a documented, verifiable setup path — from install command to rollback.

Required fields for every tool

  • Confirmed need
  • Current installation/account status
  • Supported operating system/platform
  • Exact approved version or version policy
  • Exact installation/setup sequence
  • Organization ownership and access roles
  • Configuration/environment steps without exposing secrets
  • Verification command or acceptance check
  • Update policy
  • Backup/export expectations
  • Removal/rollback instructions
  • Cost and approval owner

Tool groups

  • Development runtime/package management
  • Source control/review/CI
  • Local database/test environment if approved
  • Hosting/database/AI/communications provider CLIs if required
  • Design/print/video/media tools
  • Research/consent/support/operations tools
  • Finance/accounting/disbursement tools
  • CRM/email/campaign/analytics tools
  • Fulfillment/inventory/shipping tools
  • Password/access/incident-management tools
No unverified claims

No installation guide may claim a tool is absent or present without a current verification result.

10. Skills and Training — name a human for every capability

Competence is demonstrated, not assumed

Every capability the project needs must map to a named owner with real evidence they can do the work.

Required fields for every capability

  • Capability and confirmed workflow
  • Named owner and backup
  • Internal, partner, adviser, vendor, or unfilled
  • Evidence of competence
  • Decision authority
  • Weekly availability and dates
  • Training/onboarding needed
  • Training resource and practice scenario
  • Cost/budget source
  • Demonstration-based readiness evidence
Reading is not readiness

Reading alone is not sufficient readiness evidence for safeguarding, money movement, incident response, privacy/security, accessibility, inventory custody, or fulfillment. The people this project serves deserve practiced hands, not skimmed manuals.

Final consistency checks

  • No illustrative number is presented as approved.
  • Terminology is consistent across public copy, schema, operations, finance, and campaign materials.
  • Manual versus automated behavior is accurately disclosed.
  • Every role has a matching permission and support path.
  • Every financial promise maps to custody, approval, reconciliation, evidence, and exception handling.
  • Every physical promise maps to vendor evidence, QA, inventory, schedule, fulfillment, and contingency.
  • Every public person, quote, logo, metric, partner, and outcome has release/evidence/approval.
  • Every tool recommendation maps to a required workflow and verified setup path.
  • Every skill maps to a named coverage owner or explicit staffing/training action.
  • PDR, scope, technical requirements, stack, assets, platforms, services, crowdfunding, tools, and skills agree with the approved interview record.

When every check above holds, the package speaks with one voice — the documents in 12 through 21 can be trusted because each was verified against this schema.

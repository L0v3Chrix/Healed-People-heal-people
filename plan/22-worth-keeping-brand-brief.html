Worth Keeping does not give people their worth. It recognizes it.

This document serves every future design decision — one question decides them all: does this make the participant look like a valued contributor, or like the object of someone else's generosity?

Received from the founder 2026-07-19. This is the brand constitution: the name change, the mission, and the standard every design decision must meet. It supersedes StickerPass-era naming across all outward surfaces; historical identifiers (file names, code references) remain unchanged.

The name change

We are changing the project name from StickerPass to Worth Keeping.

StickerPass described the mechanism: a collectible sticker that connects people to local experiences and rewards. However, it did not fully express the emotional purpose or human importance of the project.

Worth Keeping communicates both the collectible nature of the artwork and the deeper mission behind the exchange.

The sticker is worth keeping — but the name is ultimately about people.

The mission

Worth Keeping creates dignity-first exchanges involving people experiencing homelessness or financial insecurity, community members, local businesses, artists, and supporting organizations.

The exchange begins when a community participant distributes a collectible sticker. This is an important reversal of the conventional charitable interaction: the participant begins as the giver.

The recipient receives artwork worth collecting and an opportunity to discover something local. Participating businesses receive community engagement and measurable participation. The person distributing the sticker contributes something desirable and valuable.

The project is designed to replace a one-directional charity interaction with an exchange in which everyone contributes and everyone receives value.

What "Worth Keeping" means

The name operates on several levels:

  • The collectible artwork is worth keeping.
  • The local experience or reward is worth discovering.
  • The human connection created by the exchange is worth keeping.
  • The participating local businesses are worth supporting.
  • Community resources should remain connected to the people who need them.
  • Most importantly, people who are frequently ignored or treated as disposable are worth seeing, caring about, supporting, and investing in.

The name expresses belonging. It says that these individuals are part of the community — not separate from it and not disposable to it.

The most important brand principle

Worth Keeping does not give people their worth.

People already possess inherent worth and dignity. The project recognizes that worth and creates a mechanism through which they can participate as contributors.

A person's value must never appear dependent on:

  • Distributing a certain number of stickers
  • Generating scans or redemptions
  • Producing revenue for a business
  • Sharing a personal hardship story
  • Appearing in campaign materials
  • Demonstrating gratitude
  • Receiving approval from a sponsor or organization

The participant is a person first. The product creates an opportunity for their existing value, creativity, agency, and contribution to be recognized.

Why the name is important

The name transforms the sticker from a promotional device into a symbol.

When someone says:

"This is for you. It's worth keeping."

the phrase refers to the artwork, but it carries a deeper message about the person sharing it.

That meaning may not be fully apparent during the first encounter. It should reveal itself as someone learns the story behind the project. This gives the brand curiosity, emotional depth, and campaign potential without forcing the mission into a literal or institutional name.

Worth Keeping should feel like a collectible art brand first. Its social importance should emerge through the experience and story.

The value exchange

Every participant should receive meaningful value.

Community participant

  • Begins the interaction as the giver
  • Distributes something desirable
  • Participates as a contributor
  • Retains agency over their identity and story
  • Can connect with appropriate resources and support

Sticker recipient

  • Receives original collectible artwork
  • Discovers the story behind the exchange
  • May unlock a relevant local experience or reward
  • Can participate without being pressured or guilted

Local business

  • Receives a meaningful community-engagement opportunity
  • Can attract local discovery and visits
  • Participates in a measurable, accountable system
  • Supports the community without exploitative messaging

Artists and creative partners

  • Create work people genuinely want to collect
  • Help build a recognizable visual universe
  • Contribute culture rather than generic charity imagery

Wider community

  • Encounters people through contribution rather than pity
  • Makes participation and connection visible
  • Helps keep attention and resources circulating locally

Brand positioning

Worth Keeping is not primarily:

  • A coupon program
  • A conventional fundraiser
  • A handout
  • A homelessness awareness campaign
  • A corporate promotional-sticker program
  • A system that turns personal hardship into advertising

Worth Keeping is:

A dignity-first collectible art experience that begins with a gift and creates a connection between people, local businesses, and their community.

Design direction

The visual identity should feel:

  • Collectible
  • Art-led
  • Curious
  • Valuable
  • Warm
  • Optimistic
  • Human
  • Slightly mysterious
  • Locally rooted
  • Culturally desirable

The work should look like something people would seek out even before learning about the mission.

The stickers must not appear to be charity tokens or promotional giveaways. They should feel like limited-edition artifacts with enough artistic quality, variation, and personality to build genuine collector interest.

Visual and messaging guardrails

Avoid:

  • Pity-driven photography
  • Images of people as helpless or anonymous
  • "Saving" or "rescuing" language
  • Poverty used as visual decoration
  • Savior-versus-recipient compositions
  • Corporate logos dominating the artwork
  • Dollar signs, cash imagery, or transactional symbolism
  • Claims that dignity is earned through productivity
  • Language suggesting that participants should prove their worth
  • Treating people as campaign assets

Prioritize:

  • Agency
  • Contribution
  • Exchange
  • Original artwork
  • Equal participation
  • Discovery
  • Local culture
  • Human connection
  • Belonging
  • Collectibility
  • Hope without sentimentality

Recommended brand language

Primary name: Worth Keeping

Founding collection: Worth Keeping: The Austin Founding Collection

Primary campaign line: The art is worth keeping. The people are worth investing in.

Exchange phrase: "This is for you. It's worth keeping."

Brand truth: Every person has value worth recognizing, relationships worth building, and potential worth investing in.

Short description: Worth Keeping creates dignity-first exchanges through collectible art, local discovery, and meaningful community participation.

Longer introduction: Worth Keeping begins with something small enough to hold: a collectible sticker offered by someone too often overlooked. The artwork is worth keeping. The local experience it unlocks is worth discovering. And the person who placed it in your hand is worth seeing, supporting, and investing in.

The standard for every design decision

Before approving any visual, message, interaction, or campaign asset, ask:

Does this make the participant look like a valued contributor — or like the object of someone else's generosity?

If the participant is not presented with agency, dignity, and meaningful value, the design is not aligned with Worth Keeping.
